1 BACKGROUND & OBJECTIVES 
Roughly one in five Canadians report experiencing social isolation (20%) and loneliness 
(19%),1 while nearly 60% indicate experiencing a lack of community connection, of which 16% 
report that they feel ‘no sense of community at all.’2 Of those experiencing social isolation, close 
to half (49%) say they feel their health is either ‘fair’ or ‘poor,’ while those reporting feeling 
frequently lonely rate their overall life satisfaction lower when compared to those who says they 
felt lonely less frequently.3 Presently in 2025, social isolation, the absence of meaningful and 
supportive relationships with other individuals,4 and loneliness, the “awareness of being 
fundamentally separated from other people and from the universe ... [via feelings of] sadness, 
hopelessness, grief, meaninglessness or anguish,”5(p1322) are a policy priorities in Canada.e.g., 6,7 
By some accounts, isolation and are an expanding “epidemic” that threatens health and society,8,9 
and one that in Canada is particularly consequential for sex/gender minority, disabled, and older 
populations, as well as those living with long-term conditions.10 Collectively, the associated 
health care costs of loneliness and isolation are significant as these conditions are leading 
contributors to Canadians’ poor mental, which the Canadian Mental Health Association estimates 
costs $50 billion annually in direct health care, lost productivity, and lost quality of life.11 
 Canadians spend almost 90% of their daily time indoors, the considerable majority of 
which is at home (69.9%).12 While 35.9% of time at home is spent sleeping,12 the centrality of 
the home environment with respect to health, well-being, early life development, and later life 
aging is increasingly well documented as a leading social determinant of health (SDoH) across 
the life course.13,14 For instance, the condition of housing (i.e., good condition v. need minor 
repairs v. need major repairs) can have disparate impacts on mental and physical health outcomes 
for residents,15 while the level of artificial lighting in the home can negatively affect indicators 
such as sleep health.16 Beyond physical and mental health implications, it is increasingly 
understood that housing can play a key role in social well-being, with reported links being noted 
to sense of community belonging,17 sense of pride,18 and social/community integration.19 
In Canada, the national housing stock has diversified across low-, mid-, and high-density 
models in recent years20 in response to urban sustainability measures (e.g., limiting sprawl), 
concerns pertaining to the loss of productive agricultural land,21 and the promotion of more 
compact and accessible economies of scale.22 However, despite the growing evidence of housing 
as a consequential SDoH with links to social health and well-being, and Canada’s diversifying 
housing mix, little is currently known about what distinct built, natural, and social designs of the 
home environment affect social health and well-being (i.e., depth, quality of one’s relationships 
with others, their neighbourhood, and their community23) as existing evidence has ostensibly 
only tracked health and well-being indicators across housing types (e.g., single family home, 
apartment) in the limited reviewed comparative studies.24 Moreover, there is a dearth of extant 
evidence-supported housing strategies to support health, and especially those that consider 
achieving key metrics of health and equity.25 The aim of this research is to support effective 
evidence-based planning and public health practice via the development of a novel, 
interdisciplinary, expert informed, and psychometrically validated ‘pro-social’ housing design 
tool for practitioners and researchers. This research seeks to achieve three/four objectives: 
Objective 1 (O1): Examine which social, built, and natural structures and features of the home 
environment among low- (i.e., 1-2 storey buildings; e.g., single family home), medium- (i.e., 3-8 
storey buildings; e.g., condos, townhomes), and high-rise (i.e., >9 storey buildings; e.g., high-rise 
apartments) dwelling residents (N=1,500) perceive as most important to their social health.

4 
 
Objective 2 (O2): Conduct a comparative multi-city (i.e., N=15/Waterloo, N=15/Edmonton, 
N=15/Montreal) photovoice study investigating resident perspectives of low-, medium-, and 
high-density housing regarding pro-/anti-social environmental design in/around their home. 
Objective 3a (O3a): Triangulate the findings of O1-O2 via expert audits to develop the ‘socially 
connective housing/home environment design – universal evaluation’ (SCHEDule) tool to 
evaluate the social design quality of different housing environments. 
Objective 3b (O3b): Validate the SCHEDule tool with community surveys featuring visuals of 
different housing models and design quality as identified in the O3.1 expert audits.

2.3 Development of the ‘SCHEDule’ tool (O3a: Oct/27-Apr/28): Synthesizing the findings 
from the O1-O3 studies will be used to create a list of assessment items reflecting pro-social and 
anti-social housing designs. For example, measures of pro-social designs may include aesthetics 
(e.g., natural amenities or spaces,27 attractive housing model or historical architecture28), active 
travel infrastructure,29 safety/crime prevention design,30 and visibility, while anti-social measures 
may feature physical hazards (e.g., narrow sidewalks,31 hard edge property designs32), social 
disconnection/lack of cleanliness (e.g., few fourth [informal] spaces33), motorized traffic/parking 
infrastructure,28 and tall fences/property dividers. The list of items will be the initial model of the 
‘socially connective housing/home environment design – universal evaluation’ (SCHEDule) tool 
which will be assessed for reliability (O4) and validity (O5) (see analytical plan in Figure X).

Recruitment: Independent raters who will test the tool will be recruited as urban planning and 
design professionals (N=15-20). In addition to being topic experts, we will aim to recruit a 
sample with an average career length of at least 5 years. Experts will be invited to complete an 
online survey wherein they will be instructed to assess the social connectivity/pro-social design 
quality—according to the developed list of items—of 45 photos featuring low-, medium-, and 
high-density housing environments (15 images per density). Participants will be given the initial 
SCHEDule tool featuring the initial pro- and anti-social design categories with descriptions of 
each item. Participants will be instructed to do their best to avoid rating the quality of image, 
weather conditions in the image, or their personal preferences about the content of the image. 
Housing Environment Images: To ensure the contemporary validity of the analysis, 45 images 
will be collected from the O3 study to represent real world examples of the pro- and anti-social 
design themes generated from the O1-O3 findings (i.e., anti-social low/medium/high-density, 
pro-social low/medium/high-density). Thus, 15 images representing housing in three densities 
from varied municipalities will be assessed by each independent rater. Raters will be instructed 
to evaluate each image for its social connectivity quality across the developed categories on a 6-
point Likert (1: Strongly Disagree, 2: Disagree, 3: Slightly Disagree, 4: Slightly Agree, 5: Agree, 
6: Strongly Agree). The rating scale will not feature a ‘neutral’ option in order to mitigate 
acquiescence bias—evaluator reluctance to respond with more extreme options which can 
contribute to central tendency biases that result in systematic error during the evaluation.34 
Reliability Analysis: Intraclass coefficients (ICC) tests will be calculated first to measure the 
homogeneity between experts when rating housing environments. ICCs indicate whether, and the 
extent to which, the scores given by raters are correlated35 (i.e., to which extent their ratings of 
the social connectivity of the housing environment increase/decrease in the same direction from 
one context to the next). ICC models will be considered as raters being picked randomly from 
the population of topic experts (random effect), and the reliability measures being based on the 
profile of their ratings. Thus, a Two-Way Random model will be used to evaluate the consistency 
of rankings (i.e., ICC, 95% Confidence Intervals) across both single items and averages per rater. 
Any items with an ICC < 0.5035 will be dropped before final evaluations of the tool structure.

6 
 
Assessments of the reliability of the structure of the initial SCHEDule tool will then be 
completed via exploratory factor analyses (EFA) and computing McDonald’s Omegas (ω). First, 
a latent variable modelling for a two-factor SCHEDule tool (pro-social, anti-social housing 
designs) will be run to collect standardized loadings (λ) and error variances (θ).36 Second, ω 
coefficients will be computed using the formula ω = (Σλᵢ)² / [(Σλᵢ)² + Σθᵢ], where: λᵢ = 
standardized factor loading for item I and θᵢ = error variance for item i. Cutoffs for factor 
loadings will be ω ≥ 0.70-0.80 (good-to-excellent) reliability37 to finalize the structure of the 
tool. McDonald’s Omegas are widely recognized as one of the most trustworthy estimates of 
reliability.38 Omegas have been favourably argued against more traditional Cronbach’s alpha (α) 
due to having fewer assumptions (e.g., unlike α, ω doesn’t require tau-equivalence [equal factor 
loadings]) and more analytical flexibility (i.e., α assumes a unidimensional factor [one factor] 
and can underestimate reliability in multidimensional scales, while ω is grounded in latent 
variable modeling which is more powerful for multidimensional scales/tools).39,40 All analyses 
will be performed in SPSS and the AMOS package.

6 ANTICIPATED IMPACTS 
This will be the first national study in Canada to explore how high-rise location, urban 
greenness, and ALE are associated with social isolation/connectedness in a national sample of 
Canadians ≥18 years. Our intersectional SGBA+ approach will help identify intersectional 
identities for whom urban design interventions might be most effective. By leveraging FCI 
support and our multi-sector city-building networks, our findings will directly inform realistic, 
equity-focused municipal policy recommendations for Canadian municipalities.
