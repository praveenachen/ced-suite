Land-Use Regulation, Municipal Infrastructure Financing, and Housing Supply and 
Affordability in Canada 
Proposal Submitted to Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC) 
1. Introduction and Purpose 
Housing supply shortages and worsening affordability remain among Canada’s most 
pressing public policy challenges. Municipal land-use regulations, planning and approval 
processes, and municipal infrastructure financing mechanisms—such as development 
charges and permit fees—play a critical role in shaping the cost, speed, and feasibility of 
new housing delivery. While these tools fall largely within municipal jurisdiction, they are 
enabled and constrained by provincial and territorial legislation and operate within 
Canada’s system of fiscal federalism. 
A central feature of Canadian municipal finance is the long-standing “growth pays for 
growth” approach, whereby new development is expected to fund a substantial share of 
growth-related infrastructure and services. While this model is intended to protect existing 
taxpayers, it may also contribute to higher development costs, slower project delivery, and 
reduced housing affordability. Policymakers at all orders of government are increasingly 
questioning whether current regulatory and financing models strike the right balance 
between accountability, fiscal sustainability and housing outcomes. 
This research project builds on existing work by CMHC. For example, in 2022, CHMC 
conducted 10 virtual roundtables with key stakeholders (planners, developers, affordable 
housing professionals) to identify barriers to affordable housing in land use planning 
systems, and to identify potential solutions.1 In 2025, CMHC published a review on the 
price impacts of development charges, and found that while new homebuyers bear the 
direct burden of development charges, spillover effects include increasing prices even for 
existing homes.2 The proposed research builds on both of these reports by deeply 
exploring where and how municipalities in Canada are overcoming these established 
barriers to creating affordable housing supply. 
The proposed research will broadly examine how land-use regulation and municipal 
infrastructure financing in Canada affect housing supply and affordability, explore the 
implications of the “growth pays for growth” model, and identify Canadian best and 
emerging practices that could support increased housing supply while remaining 
institutionally and fiscally realistic. The study is designed to inform federal, provincial, 
territorial, and municipal decision-making.

2. Research Questions 
The project will address the following key research questions: 
1) As a country, are we comfortable with the implications of using a “growth pays for 
growth” approach in municipal development financing? And what are the 
alternatives? 
This includes exploring the origins of the approach in Canada, its implications for housing 
supply and affordability, and potential alternative models within Canada’s 
intergovernmental context. 
2) What is the state of knowledge regarding the design of land-use regulation and 
municipal infrastructure financing in Canada to effectively facilitate increased 
housing supply and affordability for Canadians? 
a) How do different types of land-use regulations observed across Canada impact 
housing supply and affordability? 
b) How do different types of municipal infrastructure financing mechanisms observed 
in Canada impact housing supply and affordability? 
c) What best practices in land-use regulation and municipal infrastructure financing 
can facilitate housing supply and affordability while supporting fiscally sustainable 
growth? 
Across all questions, the research will consider: 
• Differences between market and non-market housing 
• Provincial and territorial enabling frameworks 
• Variation across municipal size, geography, and governance structures 
• Equity and inclusion considerations 
The primary outcomes of interest are housing supply (including speed of delivery) and 
housing affordability. Notably, Questions 2a, 2b, and 2c will be answered within the context 
of the state of knowledge existing in Canada, rather than to empirically evaluate these 
questions on their own.  
3. Conceptual and Analytical Framework 
The project is grounded in an applied policy framework that links: 
• Land-use regulation (e.g., planning policies, zoning, as-of-right development, 
approval timelines)

3 
 
• Municipal infrastructure financing mechanisms (e.g., development charges, permit 
fees, and related tools) 
• Developer costs, risk, and timelines 
• Housing supply and affordability outcomes 
 
The analysis situates municipal decision-making within Canada’s system of fiscal 
federalism, recognizing municipalities as constitutionally subordinate to provinces and 
territories, with limited revenue tools and varying degrees of regulatory autonomy. This 
context is essential for understanding both the “growth pays for growth” approach and the 
constraints on reform. 
To support comparative analysis, the study will make systematic use of CMHC’s Municipal 
Land Use and Regulation Index,3 which captures the relative degree of land-use regulation 
across Canadian cities. The Index provides a consistent, CMHC-developed metric that 
allows municipalities to be compared along a continuum of regulatory intensity, 
normalized relative to the Greater Toronto Area (GTA = 100). This Index will be used as an 
organizing tool for case selection and interpretation, not as a causal estimator. 
4. Research Design and Methods 
The project will be completed over ~ 10 months using five integrated workstreams. 
4.1 Advisory Team Engagement 
We will establish a Research Project Advisory Team (hereafter, Advisory Team, see 
Appendix A for a list of anticipated members), comprising senior leaders from municipal 
government, provincial initiatives, industry, planning, and non-profit housing to provide 
strategic guidance throughout the project. The Advisory Team will: 
• Validate emerging findings 
• Identify promising and problematic practices 
• Recommend key informants and municipalities for interviews 
• Provide real-world policy and implementation perspectives 
Engagement will occur at key project milestones (likely monthly meetings) rather than 
through intensive governance structures. 
4.2 Scoping Literature Review  
We will directly address Research Question 2 (What is the state of knowledge regarding the 
design of land-use regulation and municipal infrastructure financing in Canada to 
effectively facilitate increased housing supply and affordability for Canadians?) using a

structured scoping review.4,5 We will synthesize Canadian academic literature published 
since 2000 on the extent to which and how 1) land use regulations and 2) municipal 
infrastructure financing impact new housing supply, housing affordability, and adequacy of 
community infrastructure and amenities.

4.3 Environmental Scan of Canadian Municipal Practices  
An environmental scan will examine how municipalities across selected provinces are 
currently designing and implementing: 
• Land-use regulation and planning tools (e.g., zoning as-of-right, missing middle 
policies, approval processes) 
• Municipal infrastructure financing mechanisms (e.g., development charges, 
permitting fees)

5 
 
We will not attempt a comprehensive scan across all Canadian municipalities. Instead, to 
explore differences and to set the stage for understanding where and how municipalities in 
Canada are overcoming established barriers to creating affordable housing supply, we will 
leverage CMHC’s Municipal Land Use and Regulation Index3 to:  
• Classify municipalities along a spectrum of regulatory intensity 
• Support transparent and consistent selection of municipalities across jurisdictions 
• Inform the selection of municipalities for deeper qualitative analysis 
 
The Index will be used in combination with contextual information (municipal size, growth 
pressures, governance structure, and provincial context) to ensure balanced and policy-
relevant case selection. In addition, our team will ensure municipality inclusion from the 
following regions: British Columbia, Alberta, Ontario, Quebec, Nova Scotia, and selected 
Prairie municipalities for additional diversity.  
The scan will rely on publicly available municipal plans, by-laws, policies, and official 
documentation. The objective is to map variation in approaches and identify emerging 
patterns, not to rank or score municipalities. 
Results from the Environmental Scan will directly inform the selection of key informants in 
both sections 4.4 and 4.5.  
4.4 Key Informant Interviews – Thought Leaders 
Approximately 20 semi-structured interviews will be conducted with thought leaders from: 
• Academia 
• Industry 
• Municipal, provincial, and territorial governments 
• Non-profit housing organizations 
 
Interviews will be semi-structured, one-on-one virtual interviews that will build on CMHC’s 
existing work (e.g., the Barriers to Affordable Housing in Land Use Planning Systems Round 
Table Summary Report1) by structuring interview questions around the themes identified in 
the Summary Report and asking about emerging best practices in Canadian municipalities. 
The interviews will explore:  
• The effectiveness and limitations of current regulatory and financing tools 
• Experiences with and perspectives on “growth pays for growth”, and whether this 
perspective still holds merit 
• Intergovernmental dynamics and enabling conditions 
• Emerging Canadian innovations and alternatives

6 
 
The research team will obtain University of Waterloo’s Research Ethics Board approval 
prior to undertaking key informant interviews described in sections 4.4 or 4.5.  
 
4.5 Key Informant Interviews – Municipal Case Studies 
A purposeful sample of approximately 35 participants from 7 municipalities will be 
selected for in-depth interviews. Selection will be informed by: 
• Findings from the scoping literature review 
• Environmental scan findings 
• CMHC’s Municipal Land Use and Regulation Index 
• Advisory Team input 
• Thought leader key informant interviews  
• Collaboration with CMHC 
 
The sample will reflect variation in region, municipal size, urban, rural, and Northern 
contexts, and regulatory intensity and financing approaches. We anticipate conducting 
several interviews within selected municipalities to obtain a variety of perspectives from 
within each municipality. For example, some triangulation between developers, Chief 
Financial Officers/Treasurers/Directors of Finance, planners’ , and non-profit housing 
providers is warranted to tell a complete story and identify complex interactions between 
land use regulations, municipal financing mechanism, and the outcomes of housing 
affordability and supply. We will start with an initial list of potential participants, and use 
snowball sampling7 to identify additional participants within each municipality who could 
provide relevant information. 
 
Interviews will focus on: 
• Design and implementation of land-use and financing tools 
• Observed impacts on housing supply and affordability 
• Trade-offs, bottlenecks, and unintended consequences 
• Transferability to other municipal contexts 
• Implications for non-market housing providers 
5. Equity, Inclusion, and Non-Market Housing 
Equity and inclusion considerations will be integrated across all workstreams, with specific 
attention to the impact of regulatory and financing tools on non-profit and non-market 
housing developers, differential effects across regions and community types, and barriers 
faced by smaller municipalities and capacity-constrained actors.

6. Synthesis, Deliverables, and Knowledge Mobilization 
Findings from all workstreams will be synthesized to address the core research questions, 
identify best practices and trade-offs, and highlight policy-relevant lessons for all orders of 
government.  
Deliverables: 
For CMHC, our team commits to the following deliverables:  
• A final research report (maximum 40 pages), with a 2-3 page executive summary 
• Interim briefings aligned with quarterly milestones 
 
Given that the research team is comprised of academics, we also anticipate three peer-
reviewed publications arising from this project (scoping review, key informant thought 
leader paper, key informant municipality paper). We welcome a conversation with CMHC 
to discuss CMHC staff interest in co-authorship (according to ICMJE standards8). 
Final outputs will be provided in both official languages. CMHC will undertake translation, 
with an expert accuracy review conducted by the project team.
