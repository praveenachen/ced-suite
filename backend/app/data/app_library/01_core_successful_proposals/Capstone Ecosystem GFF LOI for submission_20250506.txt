Briefly describe the initiative and how it impacts one or more of the 
Global Futures.  
UW is the world’s largest co-op program, and already has the foundations to become Canada’s 
largest capstone ecosystem. Achieving that goal will require coordinating our existing efforts and 
expertise, and strategic growth in three complementary directions. 
UW currently graduates over 2,000 students per year with a mandatory capstone degree 
requirement, across four faculties: Arts, Environment, Health, and Engineering. Those 2,000+ 
students complete 500+ capstone projects per year. There are an additional ~6,000 students who 
currently graduate each year without a capstone opportunity. Currently, perhaps ~5% of these 
500+ capstone projects have meaningful connections with external partners. 
To become Canada’s largest capstone ecosystem, and realize the One Waterloo vision, UW needs 
to grow strategically in three complementary directions: (1) elective capstone opportunities for 
the ~6,000 students who currently graduate without such an opportunity; (2) apply the expertise 
we have developed running co-op at scale to running capstone Work-Integrated Learning at 
scale; and (3) similarly apply that expertise to grow the Community-Embedded Classroom 
(CEC) model.  
The five-year targets are ~3,000 students per year in a capstone course, with ~300 capstone 
projects per year engaged with external partners, and ~500 students per year in a CEC course 
(which could be a capstone course or a pre-capstone course). In the three years of this proposal 
the targets are 80% of those 5-year targets: ~2,400 capstone students per year, ~240 capstone 
projects with external partners, and ~400 students in CEC courses. 
These thousands of students and hundreds of projects will contribute to all five of the Global 
Futures. This proposal includes Global Futures Capstone Awards, amongst other aspects. New 
capstone sections can be explicitly organized around Global Futures themes. In a sense, this 
proposal is creating Global Futures Networks for undergraduates and UW’ s community partners. 
UW is currently behind our peer universities in terms of capstone engagement with community 
partners. Many of our peers (e.g., McGill, Queens, Toronto, UBC, etc.) already have developed 
capstone external partner programs in Engineering. UW can leapfrog the competition, because 
we already have larger foundations: we just need to build the superstructure and open the doors. 
UW can be the first in Canada to do this at the university level.

Describe how the initiative leverages existing and/or emerging academic 
and/or research strengths at UWaterloo. Describe, where applicable, 
how our founding differentiator strengths are being leveraged.   
As the global leader in co-operative education, UW is uniquely positioned to build on this 
foundation to scale work-integrated learning on campus through capstone projects and CEC 
convenings on campus. This proposal creates Global Futures Networks for undergraduates by 
mobilizing CEE’s foundational resources, including its WaterlooWorks platform, to support 
capstone/CEC WIL. CEE can also serve a secretariat role to streamline community and partner 
connections and communications for greater scalability across UW. 
The initiative will establish on-ramps that allow students to build capstone projects from CEC 
courses, co-op placements and experiences with Velocity, GreenHouse and the Grebel Peace 
Incubator supports and resources.  It also intentionally links capstone off-ramps that encourage 
extending student learning beyond the classroom after course completion.   
CEE’s central support will strengthen UW's entrepreneurial ecosystem by creating structured 
connections between community partners and social innovation supports like GreenHouse, 
Velocity and the Grebel Peace Incubator. It provides opportunities for internal and external 
partners to share potential capstone projects through a central platform. It will support reaching 
out to UW and external community partners—including government agencies, clinical settings, 
and industry to support CEC convenings. By partnering with Velocity, GreenHouse, and the 
Grebel Peace Incubator, the initiative ensures students receive essential mentorship and 
resources before, during and after their capstone experience. The initiative establishes clear 
pathways for students to develop entrepreneurial mindsets and translate academic projects into 
ventures through existing courses, while also providing incentives through awards for students to 
further develop their capstone projects after course completion. This creates a robust pipeline of 
innovative ideas emerging from interdisciplinary academic contexts.    
This initiative reinforces UW's commitment to regional impact by creating opportunities for 
knowledge exchange between the university and community partners on campus. It provides 
engagement pathways for community organizations, municipal governments, and regional 
businesses to participate in various ways, such as through Capstone projects or CECs. By 
expanding these collaborative learning models, the initiative strengthens UW's position as an 
engaged anchor institution committed to local impact while addressing broader challenges.  
 
Describe how this initiative involves collaboration across academic 
disciplines and organizational units at UWaterloo. 
This initiative fosters collaboration across academic disciplines and organizational units at the 
UW by integrating and enhancing a broad spectrum of previous and ongoing efforts. It aligns 
with established programs such as home capstone courses, the CEC model, and the i-Capstone 
pilot. It also creates interdisciplinary intersections with courses like ARTS450: Global 
Engagement Seminar, Wicked Problems, and WIL612: Leadership and Project Management, 
enabling graduate students to be involved as instructors and mentors in capstone courses.

Several key strategies emphasize cross-disciplinary engagement. The initiative supports a 
campus-wide convergence of capstone and CEC course schedules, allowing shared resources 
such as community partners, guest speakers, peer project reviews, and cross-course 
collaboration. Coordinating capstone projects across faculties enhances interdisciplinary 
interactions, exemplified by the upcoming collaboration between HLTH480 and BME461 in Fall 
2025. 
Efforts to integrate the capstone experience with existing academic structures extend further. 
Map the System interdisciplinary research projects facilitated by the Grebel Peace Incubator 
serve as both an introductory module and an essential learning component, while initiatives such 
as Velocity and GreenHouse provide additional support networks. The initiative also proposes 
adjustments to degree plans to remove barriers and encourage participation in capstone, with 
considerations for elective capstone opportunities as alternatives to undergraduate theses. This 
may be of particular interest to students who have not had the opportunity to engage in co-op 
(i.e., regular stream programs). 
On an organizational level, leadership is encouraged to develop mechanisms for accepting cost 
recovery payments from private and public sector capstone partners, a practice already 
established in peer institutions. Additionally, aligning capstone awards, such as the Global 
Futures Capstone Awards, with other interdisciplinary awards strengthens administrative 
cohesion and increases organizational efficiency. 
Beyond academics, the initiative emphasizes external partnerships and engagement with alums, 
creating a sustainable ecosystem for collaboration. Alum involvement enriches students’ 
experiences, while external partner engagements evolve into co-op and sponsored research 
opportunities. A unified intellectual property framework and a coordinated approach to external 
communications ensure that Waterloo’s capstone model stands out, leveraging the university’s 
strengths in co-op education and interdisciplinary innovation. 
Ultimately, this initiative builds a comprehensive, interconnected capstone experience that 
bridges faculties, institutes, students, and external partners, reinforcing the culture of 
interdisciplinary engagement at Waterloo. 
Leadership Support Items 
We are asking leadership to think differently, work together, and act with purpose in several 
dimensions:  
1. Support a common cross-selling “playbook” to have a One Waterloo way of 
articulating co-op, sponsored grad research projects, CEC opportunities and capstone. 
Capstone engagement with external partners at UW differs from capstone engagement 
with external partners at other universities because of our world leading co-op program 
and unique IP policy. We all need to be on the same page about these issues, so we can 
communicate clearly with external partners. (Work on this playbook has already begun, 
in collaboration with CEE, Advancement, and Research.)  
2. Support Alumni engagement as capstone and CEC partners. Capstone can be a 
powerful way for alumni to stay connected with UW and have rewarding experiences 
engaging with the next generation of students. 
3. Converging on a campus-wide capstone course time, so capstone courses can share 
resources: e.g., guest speakers, CEC members, peer project reviews, etc. For example, in

fall 2025 the HLTH480 and BME461 capstone courses will have overlapping times, and 
will be doing a number of activities together.  
4. Create capacity for UW to accept cost recovery payments from private and public 
sector capstone project partners. For example, we have surveyed that Engineering 
faculties at peer Canadian universities have this capacity, and charge partner participation 
fees between $1k to $4k.  
5. Supporting peer interaction opportunities between capstone courses and first year 
UCR courses. So the first year and fourth year students can give each other feedback.  
6. Supporting capstone student recruitment. UW does not currently have a system for 
recommending courses to students, short of degree plans and student advisors. We 
propose that capstone becomes a more general part of the culture, and so is aligned with 
these existing recruiting systems. In addition, we propose novel new recruiting 
mechanisms. For example, in i-capstone we have had FCI email students at the right 
academic level who have had co-op jobs aligned to cities.  
7. Create a culture of encouraging students to engage with capstone project partners. 
For example, 90%+ of the capstone projects in Engineering each year do not currently 
have a project partner, so there needs to be a gradual culture shift. 
8. Consider creating capstone course codes, if helpful to support opportunities for 
students with specific degree plans. Both i-capstone and Wicked Problems have been 
using a handful of course codes held-with each other so that students from different 
majors can get a course code compatible with their degree plan. York U. also does this. 
9. Consider updating degree plans to include an elective capstone opportunity, as 
appropriate. For example, Science is considering making capstone an alternative to 
undergraduate thesis: much of the work that could have happened in an undergraduate 
thesis can also happen in a capstone course, with a professor as the project partner.  
10. Coordinating the administration of the Global Futures capstone awards. Aligning 
with administration of other capstone awards and related awards, such as the Linda 
Carson Memorial Interdisciplinary Award.  
11. See open-ended, project-oriented upper-year courses as flavours of capstone. For 
example, the undergraduate experience in both ARTS450 Global Engagement Seminar 
and Wicked Problems is capstone-esque. See these as interdisciplinary capstone courses 
with subject themes. By coordinating them under the broader capstone umbrella, they get 
access to shared resources, external partner engagement, alignment with degree plans, 
and student recruitment. For Fall 2025, GES will be scheduled alongside the other 
capstone courses to foster interactions between students, partners, and stakeholders writ 
large. 
12. Support a common Intellectual Property framework for capstone, to be developed as 
part of this grant, consistent with UW Policy, the laws of Ontario, etc. (Preliminary work 
on this has already begun, in collaboration with the UW Office of Research.)

Where applicable, describe any existing or planned internal/external 
partners and opportunities for sustainability of the initiative beyond the 
Global Futures funding.  
This initiative ensures sustainability beyond the Global Futures funding by leveraging existing 
partnerships and cultivating new ones. Instructors have successfully drawn upon their networks 
to co-create capstone projects that align with both student interests and partner priorities. The 
cost recovery fees from capstone project partners not only support the administration of these 
programs through CEE but also subsidize the substantial expenses associated with project 
components, with engineering departments currently investing over $200,000 annually. 
Additionally, partnering with CEE to leverage existing assets like WaterlooWorks allows us to 
expand the capstone experience efficiently, eliminating the need for new systems and 
infrastructure while maximizing asset utilization. 
Enrollment levels in both i-Capstone and home capstone sections remain robust, providing a 
strong justification for teaching task allocations. These courses, often mandatory for degree 
completion, maintain steady participation, reinforcing their ongoing viability. Additionally, the 
two communities of practice will persist either as centrally funded initiatives or as service and 
teaching tasks. 
UW Centres and Institutes, including FCI and RoboHub, are crucial in fostering capstone 
partnerships. They actively engage with external partners and faculty members to identify 
potential projects and facilitate collaborations. Faculty members are also incentivized to propose 
capstone projects within the same system where external partners submit their own, enhancing 
engagement levels and ensuring diverse projects. 
The capstone model has proven to be a successful point of interaction for external partners, some 
of whom evolve into co-op employers and sponsors for graduate research projects. A notable 
example is Future Cities Institute’s partnership with Iqaluit, which initially started as a capstone 
collaboration and has since expanded to include co-op employment opportunities. 
Finally, the sustainability of the Global Futures Capstone Awards is not just a goal, but a reality. 
We have successfully developed a long-term funding model over the course of the grant period, 
securing the program's continuity and impact beyond the initial funding cycle. This success is a 
testament to the viability and impact of the initiative, and we are confident in its future 
sustainability.
