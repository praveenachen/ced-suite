Overall Goal and Objectives (3/4 page – based on Cameron’s example) 
 Since 2000, the price of a home in Canada has quadrupled with the average price now 
being around $700,000.1 Canada’s resultant and ongoing affordable housing crisis has had 
several cascading effects at the individual and interpersonal levels, as well as more broadly for 
society. Regarding the former, if the average household today were to buy the average house it is 
estimated that they would spend 60% of their disposable income on housing alone in some 
provinces,2 children in families struggling with housing affordability can experience disrupted 
development and poorer educational outcomes,3 and that those experiencing housing insecurity 
are more likely to experience exposure to communicable diseases and violence.4 Concerning the 
latter, a lack of housing combined with poor public options can precipitate crowding and 
perpetuate cycles of poverty and crime,5 and exacerbate economic inequality through reducing 
disposable income for lower socioeconomic classes to spend on other essentials such as food, 
education, and healthcare.6 Ultimately, the Canadian Mortgage and Housing Corporation 
(CMHC) estimates that to restore housing affordability in Canada, an additional 3.5 million 
housing units are needed by 2030.7  
 At the same time, extant housing strategies in Canada have been panned for applying 
definitions of ‘core housing need’ that exclude people experiencing homelessness (PEH),8 
remaining behind on integrating ‘missing middle’ (i.e., medium-density housing such as 
townhouses, duplexes, mid-rise [4-8 storeys] apartments) solutions,e.g., 9 and failing to adequately 
address regulatory obstacles.5 Moreover, with recent affordable housing projects in cities such as 
Edmonton (AB), Coquitlam (BC), and Toronto (ON) reportedly being implemented by financialized 
corporations ostensibly run by private investment returns,10 there is a need to offer municipalities across the 
country novel, typologically diverse (i.e., feature multiple types of housing options), community-oriented, 
and empirically-supported affordable/attainable housing strategies/solutions. Our partnership will answer 
the question: “How can Canadian municipalities implement an effective evidence-based, community-
oriented housing strategy that provides typically diverse attainable/affordable homes at-scale for their 
residents?” To achieve this goal, we will pursue the three following objectives over the next three years: 
O1: strengthen the impact and vision of our BUILD NOW: Waterloo Region (BNWR) team via ‘city 
sprint’ meetings to co-create an initial ‘Build Now’: Practical Attainable Housing Solutions toolkit for 
municipalities.  
O2: present our initial ‘Build Now’: Practical Attainable Housing Solutions toolkit to audiences across 
Canada and recruit new members to be involved in the revising and continued development of the toolkit. 
O3: with our expanded and national team, finalize the co-creation of an open access ‘Build Now’: Practical 
Attainable Housing Solutions toolkit that municipalities across Canada, and internationally, may to address 
their local housing issue(s). 
Originality, Significance and Expected Contribution (1/2 page – based on Cameron’s example) 
There is significant concern among policymakers and practitioners in Canada that the housing 
situation is worsening due to incrementalist approaches, bureaucratic obstacles, misaligned government 
roles, and a lack of ingenuity, resulting in a dearth of effective and timely solutions.11 Exacerbating these 
worries are the continued demographic pressures contributing to increasing housing demand. Annually, 
reports from recent years have estimated that >800,000 foreign students (study permit holders),12 >700,000 
temporary workers,13 and >360,000 new immigrants14 move to Canada. The BUILD NOW: Waterloo 
Project represents a first-of-its-kind community-led housing initiative that will produce a typologically

diverse community of attainable housing units.15 This project will leverage our team’s existing relationship 
with the Build Now project in the Region of Waterloo to build [something about Canada’s first academic-
public-developer partnership dedicated to the implementation and evaluation of a 10,000 unit attainable 
housing project]. Through strengthening our BNWR team in this project, we will create a first-of-its-kind, 
step-by-step attainable housing solution guide, based on a current project and national expert team’s input, 
for practitioners and policymakers. The development of this toolkit will also serve as a platform for future 
research regarding the co-op and mixed housing schemes and various sociopolitical, economic, 
environmental, and health indicators and topics among our team members.  
Theoretical Background (1.75-2 pages – based on Cameron’s example) 
 According to several metrics and indicators Canada is facing both a housing and 
affordable housing crisis.16 In addition to the estimated 3.5 million housing units that CMHC has 
suggested are needed by 2030,7 the Royal Bank of Canada (RBC) has observed that ~60,000 
social housing units need to be created annually to meet demand.17 Subsequently, RBC has 
recommended more investment in greater housing mix (e.g., affordable, student, rental apartment 
housing) and the implementation of legislative supports for municipalities using best (e.g., via 
Ontario’s $1.2-billion Building Faster Fund).17 Recent discussions regarding the creation of 
effective solutions and strategies from public sector,16 industry,18 academic,19 and 
nongovernmental20 actors have variously argued in favour of the need for efficiency and 
responsiveness, improved multi-sector alignment or coordination, and the further diversification 
and development the of current housing mix. 
Current ineffective attempts to address the current housing situation have led to several 
compounding issues for Canadians of many backgrounds. For instance, in the nearer term, Canada’s 
housing crisis has contributed significant financial strain to families (e.g., sacrificing basic needs like food 
and clothes),21 reduced homeownership rates and household/family formation,22 and worsened reports of 
quality of life and well-being among younger cohorts.23 In instances where there is social housing available 
but is of low-quality, there are several compounded social, economic, and health impacts that can affect 
residents. As of 2015, approximately 30% of Canadian households live in such substandard housing or core 
housing need24 where these environments have long been associated with an increased risk of asthma,25 
injury risks,26 and exposure to environmental toxins such as lead.27 
In the intermediate term, inadequate housing construction and provision has produced waitlists for 
affordable and subsidized accommodations that are unrealistic for applicants. Illustratively, in 2022, roughly 
245,900 households had been placed on social/affordable housing waitlists, with close to two-thirds 
ultimately waiting two years or longer or placement.28 In Canada’s largest cities the waitlist times are 
reaching unprecedented levels with reported waits of 15 years for a  3+-bedroom in Toronto29 and over five 
years for a rental unit in Ottawa.30 In 2025, similar trends are now being documented even in mid-sized 
cities like Windsor, Ontario where estimated wait times for social housing are five-six years long.31 In the 
long-term, with fewer younger Canadian households able to buy into the housing market and achieve 
homeownership, and subsequently build wealth but, concurrently, Canada has the highest household debt to 
disposable income ratio among G7 nations.32 In response to these many factors, emerging paradigms have 
suggested that investments in and solutions for housing in Canada need to more thoroughly engage with 
housing mix, especially as it concerns social/public stock.  
 Most of Canada’s social housing stock was built by the federal government over six 
decades ago, and has seen its level of production continue to decline since the 1980s.33 While 
Canada’s National Housing Strategy (NHS) was passed in 2017 with the mandate of investing

The BUILD NOW: Waterloo Region project is a $500-million initiative led by Habitat for 
Humanity which will build 10,000 new in the region by 2030.41 Of the existing social housing 
and affordable housing programs, none feature i) a combined housing stock and production scale 
similar, or ii) diverse housing mix, equal to the BUILD NOW: Waterloo Region project. 
Regarding the former, the BUILD NOW: Waterloo Region project is a modern version of 
Canada’s wartime housing program.38 The project explicitly seeks to build affordable units at-
scale through providing 10,000 units of ‘missing middle’ homes across the Region,37 and is will 
work with a host of partners to ensure the long-term common interest of housing in the 
community.38 With respect to the latter and housing mix, as alluded to, BUILD NOW: Waterloo 
Region will engage the community, private and public sector actors, and not-for-profit 
organizations37 to help with ensuring specific housing is provided for populations across a wide 
spectrum of incomes and needs. Notably, the project has identified designing housing for 
vulnerable populations (e.g. supportive and Indigenous housing), moderate income households, 
and larger/multigenerational households.41 Ultimately, this approach will result in the 
construction of 10,000 affordable or attainable housing units that will remain as such in 
perpetuity; feature a mix of tenure (30% Rental, 70% ownership); and use of local contractors to 
engage and build capacity in the local non-profit and co-operative housing sector.41 
Addressing the challenge of creating affordable, suitable, and sustainable home ownership in 
Canada requires a fundamental shift in how we think about and build housing. The Future Cities Institute 
proposes a world-class Living Lab in Waterloo Region to advance a transformational affordable housing 
model. Through interdisciplinary research, experiential learning, and local and international partnerships, the 
Build Now Living Lab will assess and amplify the economic, health, sustainability, technological and 
societal impacts of community-led housing — creating local solutions with global relevance. The Build 
Now Living Lab directly addresses a nationally urgent, socially complex challenge with the potential for

5 
 
real and lasting impact. It proposes a novel model of interdisciplinary, data-informed, and community-
embedded research within a large-scale real-world development. It brings together academia, civil society, 
industry, and government in a true partnership, positioning our Region as a global leader in housing 
innovation. The Living Lab will provide the evidence, tools, and scalable models needed to fundamentally 
reshape how communities are built—transforming housing from a source of crisis to a platform for 
prosperity, well-being, and sustainability. The aim of our project is to build and strengthen our 
existing Region of Waterloo Build Now team into a national collaboration that will develop a 
novel ‘Build Now’ housing toolkit for municipalities to employ for building attainable housing 
at-scale in their communities.

Knowledge Mobilization Plan (2 pages max) 
 Outputs of the proposed research project are intended for several audiences, namely those 
involved in city planning, housing, and land development decision-making processes. To this, 
end, elected officials (e.g., mayors, councillors, MPPs/MPs), urban design practitioners, 
community and public health practitioners, housing developers, and researchers (e.g., housing, 
community development, civil engineering) will be the primary targets for our knowledge 
mobilization (KMb) plans. Traditional research outputs in the form of academic articles (O2-O3) 
will be the first focus of our KMb plan. Together, the Research Team will undertake a qualitative 
studying the development and formalization of our toolkit (O2), and then complete a qualitative 
process evaluation of the partnership development (O3), which together will result in two 
manuscripts being published in peer-reviewed academic journals. Leading interdisciplinary 
social science and housing journals will be targeted for our publications including Cities (5-year 
IF: 7.5) and Housing Studies (5-year IF: 3.9). Evidence briefs covering and access to the 
developed toolkit will also be provided through our Team’s free-to-use housing policy platform 
(www.CivicInquire.com). Beyond traditional KMb plans pertaining to academic output, we will 
also leverage two additional strategies: i) international living labs partnerships and ii) local 
community engagement.  
International Living Labs Partnerships 
Our Living Lab leverages partnerships between community, government, and industry to foster 
collaboration in housing innovation. With the Waterloo Region Community Foundation 
(WRCF), we will co-host four City Sprints, a two-day design sprint developed by FCI where 
invited partners problem-solve a specific bottleneck. City Sprints will aim to develop solution 
prototypes funded through a $30K funding commitment from WRCF (no GFF funding ask). 
Second, FCI leadership meets with Build Now partners bi-weekly to ensure effective Partnership 
Engagement. Our Build Now partners have connected us to many opportunities to advance FCI 
impact in our community (in-kind external partner contribution; no GFF ask). 
Finally, Waterloo’s global presence will be reinforced through our partnership with ENoLL, the 
world-leading international organization that helps Living Labs around the world incorporate 
best practices in research and management to expand their impact. We are working with CEE’s 
International Strategic Initiatives team to identify co-op students work opportunities with ENoLL 
members. Total GFF ask $7,500 (matched 1:1 GFF:FCI) to participate in ENoLL training 
opportunities and bring world-class expertise to Waterloo. 
Community Engagement 
The research and training undertaken by the Living Lab is embedded in inclusive community 
engagement and co-creation: community engagement is foundational to research and knowledge 
mobilization, which ensures the integration of diverse voices. Finally, our team is committed to 
open data and the democratization of the tools and recommendations that are generated by this 
project. Municipalities, especially low-resource municipalities, deserve to have access to world-
class tools and information we will develop and synthesize to their own city building work.
