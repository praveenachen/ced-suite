Background 
•  Shortages in housing supply and affordability in Canada continues to be a critical and complex challenge 
involving multiple variables and key players. The speed and feasibility of new housing supply rely on 
design of land-use regulations, zoning by-laws, development approval processes, and their financing 
mechanisms (such as permit fees, development charges, and land transfer taxes).  
• The impact of municipal regulations and municipal financing mechanisms may vary across the country 
due to differences in their design and scope (CMHC 2023). But they often impact the cost of building new 
housing for developers and result in slowdown in constructing new supply of housing. The additional 
costs for developer can drive up the price for the potential homebuyer (and investors) resulting in 
worsening housing affordability for homebuyers and renters. 
• Although municipal regulations and financing mechanisms are in purview of the municipal governments, 
provincial and territorial government provide a framework to this purview through enabling legislation.  
• In Canada, municipal and local policies are intertwined with federal, provincial and territorial policies. 
Municipal and local governments receive their regulatory powers from their respective provincial or 
territorial government and in some cases, there are concurrent responsibilities. Insights on effective land-
use regulations and development financing mechanisms can help inform public policy decisions for all 
levels of government in Canada to further advance increased housing supply that is affordable to meet 
housing demand.  
• All orders of governments in Canada are taking steps to help address housing supply and affordability 
challenges across the country, however, supply shortages and costs continue to increase.  
o The Government of Canada’s Housing Accelerator Fund, launched in 2023, provides funding to 
local/municipal governments to grow housing supply while supporting affordable, diverse, and 
community resilient communities.  
o In the Speech from the Throne delivered on May 27, 2025, the Government of Canada reiterated 
its commitment to increase supply of housing to improve affordability and emphasized its desire 
to see improvements in the housing market by cutting municipal development charges in half for 
all multi-unit housing. 
o Government of Canada’s Budget 2025 emphasizes the need to accelerate homebuilding to close 
Canada’s housing supply gap, including the need to double housing starts to restore affordability. 
It emphasizes the need to modernize regulatory frameworks to speed up construction. Budget 
2025 also outlines a “generational investment strategy” for infrastructure, highlighting the 
importance of strategic infrastructure, including housing-related infrastructure among others, 
underscoring community-level benefits through large-scale national programs.  
• The adverse impact of existing land-use regulations and development funding and financing mechanisms 
on speed and affordability of new housing supply is not unique to Canada. The Housing Advisory Board 
recommendations to the European Commission (2025) recommended the need to accelerate planning 
process to reduce cost of construction. To accomplish this, the Board recommends “fundamental reforms 
of land governance systems” where “costs are more equally distributed between public authorities and 
developers” (21). 
This document outlines a proposal for a mixed-methods, exploratory study  
 
 exploring children and teens’ engagement with and behaviors associated with restaurant M2K to inform 
methods for Health Canada’s strategies to monitor restaurant M2K. Of note, the Contractor has completed the 
Women and Gender Equality Canada’s Gender-based Analysis Plus course (GBA+), and will bring an equity and 
diversity lens to all aspects of the research. Of particular importance will be to examine how gender, immigrant 
status, visible minority and Indigenous status, and rural vs. urban residence is associated with reported 
behaviors in both studies. The proposal outlines two distinct but complementary projects:  
1. A qualitative study (online, one-on-one video interviews) with children (ages 9-12) and youth (ages 13-
17) to explore how they engage with restaurant M2K, both point-of-sale and other forms of restaurant

M2K (e.g., apps, social media), and how these ways of engaging of have changed over the course of the 
pandemic. 
2. A quantitative survey, building on findings from the first project, with 1,500 children and youth across 
Canada (in all 10 provinces) to estimate the prevalence of these behaviors and engagement strategies 
with restaurant M2K for Canadian children and youth ages 9-17.  
 
Objectives and Deliverables of the Requirement 
The two projects outlined above are associated with objectives and deliverables, which are listed below.  
1. Qualitative Study Objective: To explore and report on how children and youth ages 9-17 engage with 
restaurant marketing (point-of-sale and other), the extent to which and how these ways of engaging 
have reportedly changed over the course of the pandemic, and whether children and youth anticipate 
these ways of engaging might change when pandemic-related restrictions have been lifted. 
a. Deliverables:  
i. Dataset containing qualitative data from one-on-one interviews with Canadian children and youth 
(approximately 20-30 participants, or until saturation is reached). 
ii. A report and documentation of results and themes emerging from the data analysis, with 
implications to inform M2K monitoring in Canada.  
iii. Documentation of how qualitative results will be used to inform quantitative questionnaire 
development. 
2. Quantitative Study Objective: to estimate the prevalence and frequency of behaviours associated with 
restaurant M2K (point-of-sale and other), reported changes related to engagement strategies or 
behaviours related to restaurant M2K over the course of the pandemic, and how participants anticipate 
engaging with restaurant M2K post-pandemic in a pan-Canadian sample of children and teens. A 
secondary objective of this project will be to explore participants’ knowledge and awareness of Canada’s 
Food Guide, and to explore associations between participant’s knowledge/awareness of the Food Guide 
and engagement with restaurant M2K. 
a. Deliverables:  
i. Dataset containing quantitative data from n = 1500 Canadian children and teens (ages 9-17) from all 
10 provinces (unfortunately the sample excludes participants from Canada’s territories).  
ii. A report and documentation of results from the survey, with implications to inform M2K monitoring 
in Canada.
