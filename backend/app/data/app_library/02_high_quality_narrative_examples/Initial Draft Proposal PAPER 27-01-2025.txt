1. Overview 
Project Name: 
PAPER: Planning Automation for Projects, Energy, and Retrofits 
Primary Applicant: 
Askiy Energy  
PAPER: Empowering Indigenous Energy Futures in Fort Chipewyan and Beyond 
The PAPER platform is an initiative rooted in Indigenous values and perspectives, 
addressing the systemic challenges faced by First Nations communities in the energy 
retrofit sector. By prioritizing Indigenous data sovereignty and community-led solutions, 
PAPER represents a significant step forward in creating equitable access to energy 
efficiency tools for communities with limited capacity. 
PAPER will be a solution and a Holistic Approach from an Indigenous perspective, energy 
planning is not just about technology—it's about preserving the interconnected 
relationship between people, land, and resources. PAPER reflects this holistic worldview 
by: 
1. Respecting Indigenous Data Sovereignty: Data collection and utilization in PAPER are 
guided by OCAP® principles (Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession), ensuring that 
communities retain control over their information and its use aligns with cultural

protocols. This enables governance bodies and members to have a peace of mine and 
freedom to trust PAPER with their intellectual property. 
2. Community Empowerment: Through its AI Education Program, PAPER equips local 
teams with the knowledge and skills to independently plan and implement energy retrofits. 
This approach aligns with traditional Indigenous values of capacity building and self-
determination, fostering long-term resilience. While adapting and being resilient to climate 
change. 
3. Scalable, Modular Design: The tool is adaptable, recognizing that no two communities or 
municipalities are alike. PAPER's design considers the diverse realities of First Nations 
across Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba, and beyond ensuring scalability without 
sacrificing cultural or regional relevance. 
Local Impacts: Fort Chipewyan, with its legacy as one of the oldest settlements in Alberta, 
has experienced firsthand the challenges of energy dependency and climate change. With 
its remote location, high costs of diesel dependence, and limited local capacity for large-
scale energy projects, Fort Chipewyan embodies the challenges Askiy Energy and PAPER 
seeks to address. 
1. Building on Proven Success 
   Fort Chipewyan's successful deployment of a 2.34 MW solar and 1.5 MW battery 
system—the largest off-grid project of its kind in Canada—demonstrates the 
transformative power of Indigenous-led clean energy initiatives. PAPER leverages this 
success, using automation and AI to replicate similar achievements in other communities. 
2. Addressing Capacity Gaps   
   Many First Nations, like Fort Chipewyan, face staffing shortages and technical knowledge 
barriers that delay energy projects. PAPER simplifies the process by automating data 
collection, analysis, and funding documentation, removing critical bottlenecks for 
communities with limited capacity. 
 
3. Cultural and Environmental Stewardship 
   For Indigenous communities, energy retrofits go beyond efficiency—they are about 
restoring balance. PAPER ensures that energy projects align with traditional teachings by 
emphasizing sustainability and minimizing environmental harm.

Broader Regional Benefits   
For First Nations across the Prairie Provinces, PAPER provides a pathway to:   
Reduce Dependency on Diesel: Many remote Indigenous communities rely heavily on 
diesel power, which is both expensive and environmentally damaging. PAPER streamlines 
the transition to clean energy systems, helping reduce emissions and lower energy costs.   
 
Access Funding Opportunities: By automating grant applications and funding 
documentation, PAPER bridges the gap between Indigenous communities and much-
needed financial resources.   
 
Strengthen Collaborative Networks: The platform fosters connections between 
communities, sharing success stories like Fort Chipewyan’s solar project to inspire 
others.   
Vision for the Future   
PAPER is more than a tool—it's a catalyst for self-determination, It empowers Indigenous 
communities to lead their own clean energy transitions, creating solutions that are 
culturally relevant, environmentally sustainable, and economically viable. With Fort 
Chipewyan as an anchor example, PAPER has the potential to redefine how energy 
projects are envisioned, planned, and executed across Turtle Island.   
Purpose: 
PAPER is an innovative platform designed to revolutionize energy retrofit planning for 
Indigenous communities across Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba. By automating 
data collection, analysis, and document creation, PAPER addresses the unique challenges 
of capacity limitations, data underutilization, and cost barriers. It empowers communities 
to streamline retrofit planning, access funding opportunities, and foster long-term energy 
efficiency. 
Through modular design and AI integration, PAPER enables scalable and customizable 
energy planning solutions while respecting Indigenous data sovereignty. The platform also 
includes an AI Education Program to train community teams in leveraging the tool 
effectively and independently.

Potential Organizational Structures for PAPER 
PAPER funding approach can be structured as either a For-Profit Indigenous-Owned 
Company or a Non-Profit Entity. Each structure has distinct advantages and 
disadvantages, particularly in securing funding, licensing, and ownership considerations. 
Option 1: For-Profit Indigenous-Owned Company 
Under this model, PAPER would operate as an Indigenous-owned for-profit business, 
ensuring full control and ownership of the software by Askiy Energy. 
Funding Considerations 
• PrairiesCan Contribution: If at least 51% Indigenous-owned, up to 80% of project 
costs can be funded as a repayable contribution. 
• If the project benefits the broader Indigenous community, rather than just the 
company, 50% of costs could be covered as a non-repayable contribution. 
• Stacking: Government assistance is capped at 50% for eligible capital costs and 
75% for other eligible costs. 
• In-Kind Contributions: The remaining 50% contribution can include in-kind 
contributions (e.g., from the University of Waterloo or other partners).

Pros 
   Greater Flexibility & Control: 
• Askiy retains 100% ownership of the software and all intellectual property. 
• The ability to license the software to other communities or organizations. 
• Revenue generation opportunities (e.g., SaaS model or consulting services) 
without limitations. 
   Stacking Funding Possibilities: 
• Ability to combine multiple funding sources, including Indigenous Services 
Canada (ISC), climate and energy funding, private investment, and in-kind 
contributions. 
   Business Development Funding Alignment: 
• Since this is a Business Development grant, the likelihood of securing funding as a 
for-profit entity may be higher. 
Cons 
  50% Contribution Must Be Secured Before Applying: 
• All non-PrairiesCan financing must be confirmed upfront, including any private 
investment or in-kind contributions. 
• Future revenue from licensing or operations cannot be counted as matching funds. 
• This may pose a significant challenge in securing capital before the application 
process. 
  Repayable Contribution Requirement: 
• Ideally we will be accepted under “Project that Benefits the community” so 30% is 
repayable, 50% is not. 
• If deemed that no clear benefit to community than the 80% PrairiesCan 
contribution is repayable over 5 years, starting one year after project 
completion. 
• Cash flow planning is critical to ensure loan repayment without financial strain.

Option 2: Non-Profit Structure 
In this model, PAPER would be developed under an existing or new non-profit entity, 
which would own the software and have the ability to license it to other groups, including 
Askiy Energy. 
Funding Considerations 
• PrairiesCan Contribution: Up to 80% of project costs as a non-repayable grant. 
• Stacking: If the non-profit lacks sufficient resources, government assistance can 
cover up to 100% of eligible costs. 
• Ownership & Licensing: The non-profit must retain ownership of all assets 
funded by PrairiesCan. 
• Partnership Approach: A potential structure could involve Three Nations Energy or 
another Indigenous-led non-profit partnering with Askiy Energy. 
• Askiy Energy starts a new Non-Profit entity (this could take time) 
Pros 
   80% of Costs Are Non-Repayable: 
• No requirement to repay funds, reducing long-term financial burden. 
• Less pressure to generate immediate revenue post-project. 
   Potential for Full Funding (100% Stacking): 
• If additional funding sources (federal, provincial, or municipal) are secured, the 
project could be fully funded without requiring private capital or loans. 
   Lower Upfront Capital Requirements: 
• Unlike the for-profit model, funding does not need to be secured in advance, 
making it easier to apply and qualify. 
Cons 
  Complex Ownership & Licensing Considerations: 
• The non-profit retains ownership of the software, not Askiy Energy. 
• The software could be licensed back to Askiy Energy, but this adds complexity. 
  Longer Approval Process & Bureaucracy:

• Working within a non-profit framework requires additional approvals and 
oversight, which may slow down implementation. 
• Decision-making could involve a board of directors, adding administrative 
overhead. 
  Lower Likelihood of Success with Business Development Funding: 
• Since PrairiesCan primarily funds business development projects, a for-profit 
model may have a higher chance of being approved. 
 
Recommended Next Steps 
Given the strengths and challenges of both models, the decision should be based on: 
1. Securing the required 50% match funding – If Askiy and partners can confirm in-
kind and cash contributions upfront, the For-Profit model may be preferable. 
2. Determining ownership priorities – If maintaining 100% control over licensing 
and intellectual property is essential, the For-Profit model is ideal. 
3. Balancing funding availability and application success – If obtaining the full 80% 
as a non-repayable contribution is a priority and complexity is manageable, the 
Non-Profit model should be considered. 
By carefully weighing these factors, Askiy Energy and its partners can select the best path 
forward to maximize funding, retain ownership, and ensure long-term sustainability for 
PAPER.

3. The Challenge Statement 
Key Issues: 
1. Limited Capacity in Communities: 
o Many Indigenous communities lack the internal workforce, expertise, and 
resources to manage data collection, planning, and documentation 
independently. 
o Competing priorities among staff often delay critical energy initiatives. 
2. Underutilized Data: 
o Existing energy data, utility records, and housing information are often not 
analyzed due to a lack of tools and expertise.

o Without aggregated and validated data, creating actionable energy retrofit 
plans remains a significant hurdle. 
3. High Costs and Scalability Barriers: 
o Piecemeal retrofitting approaches are costly and inefficient. 
o Bulk procurement and centralized planning require advanced tools and 
detailed documentation that many communities cannot produce. 
4. Knowledge Gaps in AI Technology: 
o Communities often lack familiarity with AI-powered tools, limiting their 
ability to leverage advanced automation for energy planning. 
 
4. Project Objectives 
Primary Goals: 
1. Automate Planning Processes: 
o Streamline data collection, analysis, and document generation to create 
high-quality retrofit plans, RFQs, and financial models. 
2. Empower Indigenous Communities: 
o Train local teams through the AI Education Program, fostering autonomy in 
managing retrofit projects. 
3. Promote Scalability and Cost Efficiency: 
o Support aggregated planning and procurement, reducing costs and 
improving implementation timelines. 
4. Ensure Data Sovereignty: 
o Develop and implement robust policies ensuring communities retain 
ownership and control over their data. 
5. Validate and Scale: 
o Demonstrate the effectiveness of PAPER through pilots in 5-10 communities, 
creating a replicable model for future use.

5. Pilot Community Journey 
The pilot will involve 5 Indigenous communities in Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba. 
1. Fort Chipewyan (Alberta) 
2. Lac La Ronge Indian Band (Saskatchewan) 
3. Frog Lake First Nation (Alberta) 
4. Pinaymootang First Nation (Manitoba) 
5. Ahtahkahkoop Cree Nation (Saskatchewan) 
Step-by-Step Journey: 
1. Community Engagement: 
o Conduct initial meetings to introduce PAPER and its benefits. 
o Define project scope, identify community priorities, and formalize a Data 
Access Agreement. 
o Deliverable: Signed agreements and recorded meeting minutes. 
2. Data Collection: 
o Perform energy audits for varied housing archetypes. 
o Collect key data, including utility usage, building characteristics, and 
occupancy patterns. 
o Deliverable: Comprehensive dataset uploaded to the PAPER platform. 
3. Data Input and Validation: 
o Input data into the PAPER platform. 
o Validate and analyze data using integrated tools like Volta Snap and CP 
Logic’s Utility Bill Analysis Tool. 
o Deliverable: Initial dashboard summarizing energy usage and retrofit 
opportunities. 
4. Plan Generation: 
o Use PAPER to generate Actionable Retrofit Plans (ARPs) and Scopes of 
Work (SOW) templates.

o Deliverable: Draft ARPs and RFQs customized to each community. 
5. Community Feedback and Adaptation: 
o Present findings to the community and collect feedback through AI-guided 
surveys. 
o Adapt plans based on community input, ensuring alignment with local values 
and priorities. 
o Deliverable: Finalized ARPs reflecting community feedback. 
6. Procurement and Financial Planning: 
o Upload contractor quotes, compare them against the SOW, and finalize a 
detailed financial plan. 
o Deliverable: Comprehensive financial documentation, including projected 
cost savings. 
7. Implementation and Monitoring: 
o Track project progress using automated reporting tools. 
o Deliverable: Final implementation report and ongoing measurement and 
verification plan. 
 
6. AI Education Program 
Goal: Enable community teams to independently use and maintain PAPER, ensuring 
sustainable energy planning. 
Program Structure: 
1. Initial Training: 
o Onboarding workshops covering data input, validation, and document 
generation. 
2. Ongoing Support: 
o AI-guided tutorials, helpdesk support, and regular webinars. 
3. Advanced Modules: 
o Training in customizing templates and analyzing trends.

Deliverables: 
• Certified training programs for community members. 
• User manuals tailored to local needs.

10. Conclusion 
PAPER represents a transformative opportunity for Indigenous communities to streamline 
energy retrofit planning while building internal capacity and fostering long-term 
sustainability. By automating critical processes and equipping local teams with AI-driven 
tools, PAPER addresses systemic barriers, reduces costs, and empowers communities to 
lead their own energy futures.
