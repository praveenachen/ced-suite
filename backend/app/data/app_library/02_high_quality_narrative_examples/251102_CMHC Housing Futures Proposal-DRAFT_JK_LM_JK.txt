Canada’s housing affordability crisis requires the rapid adoption of innovative solutions. Meeting 
these targets, however, is challenged by a construction industry facing critical labour shortages, 
making an industry-wide transformation in productivity essential. Off-site construction methods 
such as prefabrication could significantly accelerate housing supply, but its potential is often 
constrained by municipal approval processes that were designed for conventional, on-site 
construction timelines. While these legacy processes are well-intended to ensure safety and 
quality, they are not yet structured to support the parallel, manufacturing-based workflows that 
make prefabrication efficient. This creates a structural misalignment, resulting in bottlenecks and 
delays that hinder a key housing innovation. 
This project will address this critical operational gap by developing a standardized, digitally enabled 
pathway for the approval of prefabricated housing development. To achieve this, our objectives are: 
1. To map the current approval journey for prefabricated construction from the applicant's 
perspective in the cities of Waterloo and Ottawa, documenting key timelines and 
bottlenecks that prevent parallel work streams. 
2. To document the municipal approval processes of three BC Housing/DASH Demonstration 
projects in Prince George, Victoria and Abbotsford (https://www.acceleratedhousing.ca) 
and validate the identified bottlenecks and assess the time savings achieved by accelerated 
digital approval methods. 
3. To identify specific opportunities where digital tools, particularly Building Information 
Modeling (BIM), can be integrated into municipal workflows to streamline document 
submission, automate compliance checks, and improve inter-departmental coordination.

2.1 Background and Justification of the Project 
Canada is facing a critical housing affordability crisis, with the federal government setting 
ambitious targets to accelerate development. To restore affordability, Canada’s housing starts need 
to reach 480,000 units annually until 2035 (CMHC 2025). Meeting these targets presents a dual 
challenge of scale and capacity. The construction industry's existing labour shortages and 
plateauing productivity mean traditional methods are insufficient to build at the required pace, 
creating an urgent need for transformative innovations (Haas et al. 2025). Methods such as 
prefabricated and modular construction are essential for achieving this transformation, offering the 
potential for faster, higher-quality, and more sustainable housing (Ezcan et al. 2025). 
Despite this potential, the adoption of these modern construction techniques is severely hampered 
by a fundamental structural misalignment. Existing municipal review and approval workflows are 
designed for the linear, sequential timeline of conventional on-site construction. These legacy 
systems are not yet structured to support the parallel, manufacturing-based workflows that are the 
hallmark of prefabrication's efficiency. The core challenge is the lack of a standardized pathway 
that allows off-site factory work to begin concurrently with on-site preparations. This misalignment 
creates systemic bottlenecks and delays, preventing a key housing innovation from reaching the 
required scale. Inconsistent approval processes and fragmented code jurisdictions are also key 
challenges to be overcome (Haas et al. 2025; Altus Group 2022).

2.2 Alignment with CMHC Focus Areas 
This project aligns directly with Focus Area 3: Building Science and Construction Innovation. It 
addresses several key topics identified in the Call for Proposals by systematically identifying the 
barriers preventing the adoption of housing innovation. Our central goal is to define the optimal 
municipal processes necessary to scale prefabricated housing contributing meaningfully to closing 
the housing supply gap. Furthermore, we directly investigate how BIM can serve as a digital tool for 
scaling up these innovations within a modernized municipal framework. 
2.3 Current State of Knowledge/Research Gap 
The benefits of BIM for coordination and efficiency in the design and construction phases are well-
documented (Succar et al. 2025; Jia et al. 2018). However, little empirical research has focused on 
how BIM can be integrated into municipal review and approval workflows for prefabricated housing 
development. This gap is significant, as municipalities currently rely on 2D-based plan and drawing 
reviews that are manual, time-consuming, and prone to inconsistent interpretation (Altus Group 
2022). This project will investigate how BIM can solve these problems by providing a single source 
of truth for all stakeholders and creating the opportunity to automate compliance verification, 
moving beyond problem diagnosis to develop a solution-oriented, actionable framework. 
2.4 Overall Goal and Specific Objectives 
The overall goal is to address a critical operational gap by developing a standardized, digitally 
enabled pathway for the approval of prefabricated housing development. Our specific objectives 
are: 
1. To map the current approval journey for prefabricated construction from the applicant's 
perspective in the cities of Waterloo and Ottawa, documenting key timelines and 
bottlenecks that prevent parallel work streams. 
2. To document the municipal approval processes of three BC Housing/DASH Demonstration 
projects in Prince George, Victoria, and Abbotsford (https://www.acceleratedhousing.ca) 
and validate the identified bottlenecks and assess the time savings achieved by accelerated 
digital approval methods. 
3. To identify specific opportunities where digital tools, particularly BIM, can be integrated into 
municipal workflows to streamline document submission, automate compliance checks, 
and improve inter-departmental coordination. 
4. To develop a scalable, best-practice guide and standardized digital workflow that provides 
municipalities with a clear roadmap for managing the unique challenges of off-site 
construction approval processes while ensuring regulatory quality. 
2.5 Methodology 
To achieve the project's objectives, we will employ a multi-stage, mixed-methods approach. 
1. Mapping the Approval Journey (M1): We will map the approval journey from the applicant's 
perspective by first creating detailed swim lane diagrams based on municipal documents 
(e.g., by-laws, permit applications) that illustrate how applications flow between key

departments and identify handoffs and dependencies. Upon receiving ethics approval, 
these initial maps will be validated through semi-structured interviews with municipal staff 
and industry applicants to identify specific procedural barriers to parallel construction 
workflows. 
2. Analyzing Bottlenecks and Integrating DASH Data (M2): We will integrate process data from 
the BC Housing/DASH Demonstration projects (Prince George, Victoria, and Abbotsford) to 
establish real-world performance benchmarks. The validated process maps will be 
analyzed using Value Stream Mapping (VSM) to pinpoint non-value-added steps, quantify 
wait times, and identify rework loops. This analysis will be supported by a comparative 
bottleneck analysis, incorporating time and motion studies and critical path methods to 
quantify delays and identify timeline dependencies. This integrated analysis will provide the 
empirical basis for addressing the need for efficiency. 
3. Developing the Digital Framework and the Proof of Concept (M3):  Using the data from M2, 
we will determine where BIM can most effectively automate compliance verification and 
streamline data flow. We will then develop a Proof of Concept (PoC) by using a BIM model 
and project documentation (sourced from a collaborator or a sand-box model) to test 
automated digital review. This PoC will validate a critical subset of compliance 
requirements essential for concurrent off-site approval, generating a concrete, scalable 
example for the proposed digital framework. 
4. Synthesizing and Validating the Harmonized Framework (M4): The insights from the previous 
methods will be synthesized into a draft of the harmonized digital framework and best-
practice guide. This draft will be developed through iterative consultation with municipal 
and industry experts to ensure its content is relevant and practical. The final framework will 
then be validated through focus groups with municipal planners, engineers, and 
administrators to confirm its implementation feasibility, robustness, and potential for 
adoption. 
2.6 Anticipated Results, Significance, and Contribution 
The project will produce key results: detailed process maps illustrating the procedural 
dependencies that currently prevent parallel workflows, a comparative bottleneck analysis, a 
harmonized digital framework, and best-practice guide informed in part by following the DASH 
Demonstration projects through the approvals process in real time (SvN 2024). This guide will 
provide municipalities with an actionable model for integrating digital tools and managing the 
concurrent review and approval streams necessary for off-site construction. By creating a clear 
pathway for parallel approvals, our framework will unlock prefabrication’s efficiency, reducing 
timelines/costs and accelerating the delivery housing supply. This work will make a significant 
contribution by providing a scalable model for modernizing municipal workflows to support 
innovative construction methods, aligning with national goals for housing and economic 
competitiveness.

Section 3: Workplan and Timelines (maximum 250 words)  
Provide a workplan detailing all activities, major milestones and outputs produced at each 
milestone. Outline any potential challenges in project implementation and strategies to address 
them. 
This 12-month project is structured across five phases. 
• Phase 1: Foundational Mapping (Months 1–4). We will submit for ethics approval while 
concurrently conducting documentary analysis of procedures in Waterloo and Ottawa. This 
will create swim lane diagrams to map the prescribed approval journey. 
• Phase 2: Validation & Interview Analysis (Months 5–8). Upon receiving ethics approval, we 
will conduct interviews to validate initial maps. We then will analyze interview data, 
quantifying barriers and compiling validated process maps. 
• Phase 3: DASH Data Integration & VSM Quantification (Months 5–10). We will integrate data 
from the DASH Demonstration projects to establish real-world performance benchmarks. 
This will involve VSM and comparative bottleneck analysis to produce quantified delays and 
dependencies. 
• Phase 4: Framework Design & PoC (Months 8–11). We will synthesize the quantified data 
from Phase 3 to design a draft digital framework architecture. We then will develop the BIM 
PoC using DASH project data to test automated digital review and validation. 
• Phase 5: Validation & Dissemination (Months 11–12). In the final months, we will synthesize 
all findings into a best-practice guide and validate the draft framework architecture through

Section 4: Results and Knowledge Mobilization (maximum 250 words)  
Discuss how the research results are likely to be used, what their expected outcomes might be and 
any obstacles to the eventual use of the results. Specify how the use of the results will help 
advance the strategic objectives of this call, especially ways new insights and evidence may drive 
decision-making, stimulate dialogue and improve outcomes for the Canadian housing system. 
To ensure our research translates into actionable change, we will implement a multi-channel 
knowledge mobilization plan in partnership with the BC Housing Research Centre which is a co-
creator of the DASH Initiative. The project's primary output will be a harmonized digital framework 
and best-practice guide, providing municipalities with a practical model for managing the 
concurrent review and approval streams essential for off-site construction. This will be supported 
by our detailed process maps and comparative bottleneck analysis. 
Our knowledge mobilization strategy is tailored to key stakeholders: 
• For Municipal Practitioners: The guide will be the primary tool, supplemented by direct 
engagement with our partners in Waterloo (two-tier municipality) and Ottawa (single tier 
municipality) through workshops to ensure the framework is practical and can be readily 
implemented. We will collaborate with the Association of Municipalities of Ontario to host a 
webinar with their members to introduce the guide.  
• For the Broader Housing Sector: Findings will be disseminated widely through presentations 
at key industry and academic conferences and a publication in a high-impact, open-access 
journal to ensure the model is accessible to all. 
• For Policy-Makers: We will produce a targeted policy brief that translates our findings into 
high-level recommendations for creating provincial and federal policies that support the 
municipal adoption of the standardized digital workflows. 
All materials will be publicly available on the Future Cities Institute website. Finally, we will deliver a 
comprehensive final report and presentation to CMHC to ensure the results inform Canada's 
national housing strategy.
