A-4.1a Background (What problem needs to be solved?) 
Provide an overview and describe the main activities of the partner organization. Explain the

Overview of partner organization  
The Greater Kitchener Waterloo Chamber of Commerce (GKWCC) acts as a strong advocate for 
continued community development and a partner for prosperity by connecting, educating, and 
developing the business community through networking events, educational programs, and 
advocacy. A portion of the GKWCC’s advocacy efforts involve working with all levels of government 
on issues related to taxation, business regulations, land supply, economic development and 
essential public services. Of note, the GKWCC’s Physician Recruitment efforts have helped bring 
150+ family physicians to the Region, demonstrating their commitment to community development 
beyond traditional business advocacy. 
 
Together with the Business and Economic Support Team of Waterloo Region (BestWR), the GKWCC 
is advancing its advocacy efforts through the development and implementation of the Vision One 
Million Scorecard. 
 
Description of current challenge  
The Waterloo Region stands at the threshold of massive growth, with projections indicating that the 
Region’s population will grow from approximately 678,000 residents (as of year-end, 2024) to one 
million by 2050 — an increase of over 300,000 people. Currently, our housing supply, transportation 
infrastructure, healthcare services, employment opportunities, and social infrastructure are not 
advancing at the pace needed to support this population boom. Local leaders recognize that the 
Region’s current infrastructure is not advancing at the pace needed to support this expansion, 
creating an urgent need for coordinated monitoring and planning.  
 
In response, the Greater Kitchener-Waterloo Chamber of Commerce (GKWCC) and the Future 
Cities Institute (FCI) at the University of Waterloo are developing and implementing the Vision One 
Million Scorecard (the “Scorecard”), a unified framework that measures infrastructure readiness 
across five key pillars that are vital to regional operations and community support. These include: 
housing, transportation, healthcare, employment, and placemaking.   
 
The team has conducted an expansive review across the five key pillars listed above to understand 
what it takes to support a population of one million people. This involved reviewing all aspects of 
community and population growth, leveraging the knowledge and experience of metropolitan areas 
across the globe, and reflecting on how the needs of the Region of Waterloo may differ from other 
communities.

In general, current efforts to assess growth readiness remain inconsistent and fragmented across 
industries and sectors, making it difficult to develop a comprehensive understanding of the 
infrastructure requirements necessary to support the Region of Waterloo's projected growth. This 
project aims to address this critical gap in growth readiness monitoring by: 
 
1. identifying evidence-based approaches for municipalities to measure infrastructure 
readiness,  
2. establishing benchmarks and targets that align with best practices while accounting for the 
region's unique context and needs, and  
3. developing a framework that can be adapted and scaled to other municipalities facing 
similar growth challenges. 
 
By addressing the interconnected social, economic and political challenges inherent in preparing a 
Region for tremendous population growth, the Scorecard will promote accountability, informed 
decision-making and coordinated action.  
 
Impact of project 
This project will significantly enhance the GKWCC's advocacy efforts by establishing a long-term, 
data-driven infrastructure monitoring system that will inform and strengthen how the Region 
prepares for and supports sustained population growth. The Scorecard creates an enduring 
framework that transcends short-term priorities and individual initiatives, positioning the GKWCC 
as a convener of cross-sectoral collaboration that brings together municipal governments, 
academic institutions, community organizations, and civil society around shared metrics and 
collective responsibility for outcomes. 
 
By providing a clear and accurate picture of which infrastructure investments should be prioritized 
to support municipal growth, the Scorecard will enable coordinated action across all sectors, 
transforming population growth from a resource worry to an opportunity for the Region of Waterloo. 
This positions the GKWCC as a key player in driving this transformation while contributing 
meaningful knowledge to the broader field of municipal growth planning through a scalable 
framework that can be adapted by other regions experiencing similar population growth. 
 
Expertise required to solve the problem 
Specialized expertise required to develop this solution includes (but is not limited to):  
• Proficiency in data collection and analysis 
• Experience working across interdisciplinary teams with diverse subject matter expertise 
• Ability to integrate multiple perspectives and adapt communication approaches for 
different stakeholder groups

Impact on Society 
The BestWR Scorecard project, specifically pertaining to process documentation, will be invaluable 
in supporting communities across Canada as they prepare for population growth in their 
communities. This project will provide a clear process, as well as completed product, demonstrating 
how communities can find and solve resource gaps through community engagement and 
partnerships.  The completed Scorecard acts as a “proof of concept” document, whereas the 
progress documentation forms a “do it yourself” guide.  Through this, the growth and betterment of 
communities across Canada will be supported.  
 
Importance 
This contribution is important because the population size  and demographic makeup o f many 
communities in Canada are changing. For example, Waterloo Region has become the fastest -
growing metropolitan area in Canada over the last 10 years , as well as the youngest. Even still, our 
population is aging, and consideration needs to be placed on how the community wishes to develop 
infrastructure and resources going forward. This story is playing out in many other communities 
across the country, and it will be beneficial to be able to learn from our findings.

6.2  DECLARATIONS FROM APPLICATION FORM 
A6.2a Conflict of interest 
 
Mitacs will not approve a BSI application  if the intern listed on the proposal has a position of 
ownership, employment, or influence over the daily operations of the partner organization. Interns 
with close family or intimate relationships with academic supervisors or employees of the partner 
organization are also ineligible. Any other real or perceived conflict of interest (COI) - for example, 
past intern employment at the partner organization  - must be disclosed to Mitacs during the 
application process using  the Intern Eligibility and Conflict of Interest Declaration Form. Academic 
supervisors must disclose any COIs through the Academic Institution Acknowledgement of COI(s) 
Form, and submit this documentation on behalf of their post -secondary institution when applying 
through the Registration and Application Portal (RAP) .  
 
Mitacs will first determine if BSI program rules are satisfied, and if so Mitacs will then review the 
COI to determine whether mitigations can be enacted to manage the COI. If a COI does exist, we 
suggest that you contact your Mitacs Advisor prior to starting an application to ensure that the 
participants in this project are eligible for the program. Please view the Mitacs Conflict of Interest 
Policy for more information.    
 
PLEASE NOTE : Failure to disclose a COI will :  
• Delay the project  evaluation proces s. 
• Require submission of a COI mitigation approach  satisfactory to Mitacs  before project work 
initiates or continues.  
• Risk forced withdrawal from the BSI program , should a satisfactory mitigation plan not be 
reached.
